# Engagement Patterns — Agent Reference

This file defines patterns for two engagement modes: Comment Assistant (responding to other people's posts) and DM Assistant (outreach messages). Plus CTA patterns for adding newsletter/offer plugs to posts.

---

## Mode 1: Comment Assistant

### What Good Comments Do

A strong LinkedIn comment:
- Adds a unique angle not already in the post
- Reads like a quick thought from a smart colleague, not polished marketing copy
- Is 2-3 sentences max
- Focuses on one specific aspect of the post

### Comment Types

**Counterpoint**: Respectfully challenges or nuances the post's claim.
> "Honestly, I've seen the opposite work too — especially in early-stage companies where consistency matters more than optimization. The goal is reps, not perfection."

**Additive insight**: Adds a related observation the post didn't cover.
> "One thing I'd add: the timing matters as much as the format. I've seen the same post get 10x engagement on Tuesday vs. Sunday."

**Personal reflection**: Brief first-person take that resonates with the content.
> "This hit. I spent two years 'building an audience' without ever defining who it was for. Changed the whole game when I got specific."

**Tip**: Specific, actionable thing the commenter learned.
> "The fastest way I've found to do this: set a 10-minute timer, write everything, then delete the first paragraph. First paragraph is almost always setup."

**Mistake identification**: Names something common that the post implies but doesn't say.
> "Most people skip step 3 entirely. They optimize the output without ever fixing the input — which is why the results don't stick."

### Comment Voice Rules

- First-person throughout
- Use contractions: "I've", "don't", "isn't", "here's"
- Limit exclamation points — sounds too enthusiastic
- Use conversational phrases: "honestly," "I think," "you know," "I mean"
- Short sentences mixed with occasional longer thoughts
- Casual punctuation: dashes, ellipses...
- Sound like you're quickly responding, not crafting prose

**Forbidden in comments:**
- "Great post!" (lazy)
- "This really resonates with me!" (no substance)
- "Well said!" (adds nothing)
- Generic agreement without a perspective

### Comment Generation Process

When user pastes a post to respond to:

1. Read the full post
2. Identify 3 distinct angles (counterpoint / additive / reflection)
3. Generate one comment per angle
4. Label each with its type
5. Each comment = 2-3 sentences only

## Mode 2: DM Assistant

### The 3-Part DM Structure

Every cold or warm DM follows this structure:

1. **Compliment or personal note** — Specific to them, not generic
2. **Problem-related question or statement** — Shows you understand their world
3. **Soft ask** — "Mind if I share more?" or "Would that be useful to you?"

### Research Framework

Before writing a DM, research the target by asking:

**3 common challenges** they likely face (based on their role/channel/industry)
**3 unique insights** you could share that would catch their attention
**3 personalized compliment ideas** based on their typical content or positioning

### DM Examples by Persona

**Cold outreach to a content creator:**
> "Your post on content batching last week was one of the more practical takes I've seen on that topic — especially the bit about voice notes.
>
> I've been noticing a lot of creators struggle with the gap between consistent posting and consistent quality. Not a volume problem, more of a clarity problem.
>
> Mind if I share a quick framework I've found helps with that?"

**Warm follow-up after engagement:**
> "Appreciate you engaging with my post on positioning — your comment actually made me think about it differently.
>
> I work with consultants who are dealing with exactly what you described. Usually it comes down to one thing they keep overlooking.
>
> Would it be useful if I shared what that tends to be?"

**Industry-specific cold DM:**
> "I've been following your work in [space] — particularly the way you've approached [specific thing]. Clear, no-fluff. Rare.
>
> One pattern I keep seeing in your space is [specific challenge] — it's subtle but it compounds over time.
>
> Would it be valuable if I broke down how others are solving it?"

### DM Rules

- Never paste the same DM to multiple people — each must feel personal
- The compliment must be specific (reference something real, not "I love your content")
- The problem statement must resonate without sounding like you're selling
- "Mind if I share more?" is the safest soft ask — it gives them control
- Do NOT ask to jump on a call in the first message
- Keep the full DM under 100 words

### DM Generation Process

Ask the user for:
1. Role/persona of the person they're messaging
2. Industry the target is in
3. What channel/content type the target uses (email list, LinkedIn, lead magnets, etc.)
4. What problem the user's offer solves

Then output:
- 3 challenge observations
- 3 unique insights to share
- 3 personalized compliment ideas
- 3 DM variations using the 3-part structure

## CTA Plug Patterns

Use these when adding a newsletter or offer CTA to the end of a LinkedIn post.

### The Classic
> "If you love ideas like this, you'll love my newsletter.
> Every [cadence], I share [2-3 words on what subscribers get].
> Join [X] readers here: [URL]"

### The Credibility
> "By the way, I [credibility statement 1].
> And [credibility statement 2].
> Want more on [topic]? Subscribe for [cadence] updates: [URL]"

### The Benefits
> "If you're looking to [outcome], my newsletter is for you.
> I'll show you how to:
> - [Benefit 1]
> - [Benefit 2]
> - [Benefit 3]
> Join [X] other subscribers here: [URL]"

### The Pivot
> "I love [point related to post].
> Over the last [X] years, I've [credibility].
> That's how I [outcome].
> Subscribe for [cadence] updates on [topic]: [URL]"

### The Proven Path
> "The quickest way to [outcome]:
> [Topic related to post].
> This is how I [achievement 1], [achievement 2].
> Every [cadence], I share [type of content] on [topic].
> Join [X] readers here: [URL]"

### CTA Rules

- Only one CTA per post
- CTA goes at the very end, after the closing question
- Match the CTA style to the post's tone (casual post → The Classic or The Pivot; authority post → The Credibility)
- Never include both a question and a CTA on the same final line — one or the other
