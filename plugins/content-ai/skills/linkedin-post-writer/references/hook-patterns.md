# Hook Patterns — Agent Reference

Hooks are the first line of any LinkedIn post. They must be 9 words or fewer. The job of the hook is to create enough curiosity that the reader clicks "See More."

The first 125 characters are visible before the cutoff. The hook must end with an incomplete thought, a curiosity gap, or a cliffhanger — never a complete thought.

---

## The 6 Single-Sentence Hook Types

### Type 1: Declarative

State something bold and true. No hedging.

**Pattern:** [Strong claim about how something works]

**Examples:**
- "Becoming successful doesn't just happen."
- "Going to the gym isn't a hobby, it's a lifestyle."
- "The key to project management isn't a tool — it's your ability to listen."
- "You can't change the world without first changing yourself."

**When to use:** Starting a how-to, a lessons post, or a steps post.

---

### Type 2: Thought-Provoking Question

Ask something the reader hasn't considered, or something that reveals a gap they have.

**Pattern:** [Question that makes reader stop and think about themselves]

**Examples:**
- "Did you know the average Millennial has less than $10,000 in savings?"
- "What's the difference between someone who goes pro versus stays amateur?"
- "If you've been thinking about writing online, was your first impulse to start a blog?"

**When to use:** Opening a stats post, a case study, or a hot take.

**Rule:** The question must have a non-obvious answer. Don't ask things that are easy or rhetorical in a generic way.

---

### Type 3: Controversial Opinion

State a take that a significant portion of your audience will disagree with.

**Pattern:** [Contrarian claim about something widely accepted]

**Examples:**
- "It's impossible to be both rich and happy."
- "Intelligence isn't something you're born with."

**When to use:** Hot take posts, X vs Y posts. Use sparingly — burn too much and you lose credibility.

---

### Type 4: Moment in Time

Drop the reader into a specific scene or moment. Creates immediacy.

**Pattern:** [Specific time/date/moment] + [what happened]

**Examples:**
- "In 1982, David Ogilvy wrote an internal memo titled 'How to write.'"
- "My fear of cafeteria trays began in 8th grade."
- "When I was 17, I became one of the highest-ranked WoW players in North America."

**When to use:** Story posts, case studies, lessons posts. Any time a personal moment anchors the insight.

---

### Type 5: Vulnerable Statement

Open by admitting something most people in your position wouldn't admit.

**Pattern:** [Personal admission that reveals struggle or limitation]

**Examples:**
- "By the time I closed my first company, I'd lost $10M of investor money."
- "For the first 10 years of my career, I was a terrible employee."
- "I don't know anything about investing (nada, zilch)."

**When to use:** Story posts, personal lessons, anything where trust-building is the goal.

**Rule:** The admission must be real. Fake vulnerability reads as fake.

---

### Type 6: Weird Insight

Open with a fact or observation that is surprising, counterintuitive, or just odd enough to make someone stop.

**Pattern:** [Unexpected or little-known fact that relates to your topic]

**Examples:**
- "Did you know 85% of the world's oxygen comes from phytoplankton?"
- "California is not the largest US state — Alaska is."
- "Crows are one of the most intelligent animals on earth."

**When to use:** Stats posts, case studies. Best when the weird fact actually connects to the main point.

## The 16 Hook Formats (Proven Hook Generator)

These are more structured templates. Use when you have the topic and need to generate multiple options.

| Format | Name | Opening Structure |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| 1 | If...Then Algorithm | "{X} 'If, then' algorithms for {Topic}:" |
| 2 | Brutal But True Story | "The first {Time Period} of my {Activity} were brutal. {Negative outcomes, comma-separated}." |
| 3 | Average Person Is Wrong | "The average person thinks {Common Belief}. They're wrong:" |
| 4 | Curated Opinion | "The problem with {Undesirable Behavior} in your {Area}: - {Negative Consequence}" |
| 5 | One Habit, Huge Outcome | "The one habit that {Huge Outcome}: {Description of Habit}. But most people waste years trying to {Action}." |
| 6 | The How To Post | "How to {get 'result'} {this week/now}. ({Steal my framework at the end})" |
| 7 | What Is It Really About | "Most people think {Topic} is about {X}. It's not." |
| 8 | Belief Changer | "[State belief that's about to get challenged]" |
| 9 | Here's How I Do It | "I {do surprising thing}. Here's my exact process:" |
| 10 | Stop/Start | "Stop {common behavior}. Start {better behavior}." |
| 11 | Investment In Myself | "The best investment I made this year wasn't {obvious thing}." |
| 12 | Truth Nobody Accepts | "{Uncomfortable truth nobody says out loud}." |

## Hook Rules

1. **9 words or fewer for the first line.** Count them.
2. **No complete thoughts.** End with a colon, ellipsis, or mid-tension.
3. **No emojis in hooks** unless the user's voice template specifically uses them.
4. **Don't use the post's title as the hook.** The hook is a door, not a label.
5. **Generate 3 hook options** for any post. Let the user pick.
6. **Match the hook type to the post format.** Story posts → moment in time or vulnerable. Stats posts → question or weird insight. Hot takes → controversial opinion or declarative.

---

## Hook Generation Process

When generating hooks for a specific topic:

1. Identify the post format being used
2. Generate one hook from each of the 3 most appropriate hook types
3. Label each with its type
4. Note word count for each
5. Present all 3 and ask user which direction to take
